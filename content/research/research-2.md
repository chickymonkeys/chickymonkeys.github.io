The rise of populist parties often lies on the aftermath of financial crises. However, while the literature focuses on the impact of economic changes in trade or unemployment on populist voting, evidence on the causal effect of banking crises is scant due to the difficulties in disentangling it from other factors. In this paper, we aim to fill this gap. We investigate whether lending cuts by banks increase the electoral support for populist parties. To this end, we exploit the impact of exogenous lending cut by a large German bank in 2007-08 on the voting behaviour of individuals set in counties exposed to the cut. We use the exposure of each county to the lending cut as an instrument, since it relies on a breaking-up of the governance sites after World War II, driven by strategic -  rather than economic - considerations. We combine this data with survey data on individual voting intentions for different parties, including the right-wing populist party Alternative Für Deutschland. Based on this strategy, our paper shows new empirical evidence on the causal effect of banking crises on individuals’ political preferences.
