Does the experience of a banking crisis influence households’ borrowing behaviour? We investigate this question by focusing on households that migrated to Germany between 1950 and the 2007 financial crisis. Combining data on migrants from 123 different countries with data on systemic banking crises, we identify the impact of the crisis' experience through the variation in timing of arrival. We compare the loan portfolios of households with a household head that experienced a systemic banking crisis in her country of origin with those that did not. We find that household that witnessed at least a crisis earlier in their life are less likely to undertake private loans than those that never experienced a crisis. Moreover, we show that the probability of borrowing is lower for migrants who experienced multiple crisis compared to those that experienced a single episode, suggesting a cumulative effect of banking shocks.
